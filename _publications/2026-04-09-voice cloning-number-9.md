Rapid voice cloning enables the creation and sharing of Human Voice Cloning Agents (HVCAs), pushing this technology into public use and raising complex social and ethical issues alongside significant attention. This study investigates user experiences, perceptions, and impacts during HVCA creation, sharing, and interaction. Employing interviews, survey, and social media analysis, the research provides multi-dimensional empirical insights. Findings indicate HVCA sharing creates complex trust dynamics (familiarity vs. risk concerns), blurs human-AI boundaries affecting relationships, and poses challenges in voice identity control, privacy, and ethical responsibility. This research offers initial empirical insights into HVCAs in user-driven social contexts, highlighting unique trust, privacy, and ethical challenges. The findings have crucial implications for responsible HVCA design, platform policies, and regulation, emphasizing enhanced control, privacy, ethics, and oversight.
